---
title: "ADR 0002: 7-day account-deletion grace window"
description: "Why account deletion is reversible for 7 days before a scheduled purge."
---

**Status:** Accepted

## Context

GDPR-style "right to erasure" requires that users can delete their account and have their data actually removed on a predictable schedule. But instant, irreversible deletion is dangerous: it makes accidental clicks, account compromise, and coercion permanently destructive. We needed a flow that guarantees erasure while protecting against irreversible mistakes - at zero operating cost.

## Decision

A **7-day grace window** with a scheduled purge:

1. The user requests deletion and re-types their username to confirm; the server re-verifies it against the live record.
2. The account is scheduled for purge 7 days out, and an email with an **undo** link is sent.
3. During the window, the undo link cancels the deletion.
4. After 7 days, a cron job purges every row. A short post-purge retention window remains only so the completion email can be delivered, then the deletion record itself is dropped.

## Alternatives considered

- **Immediate hard delete** - simplest and most "private," but accidental or coerced deletions are unrecoverable, and there's no audit trail for the completion notice. Rejected.
- **Soft delete with no purge** - reversible forever, but never actually erases data, failing the erasure guarantee. Rejected.
- **30-day window** - a longer cushion, but it keeps personal data around longer than necessary. The legacy 30-day value is retained only as a final backstop constant, not the active grace period.

## Consequences

- Erasure is guaranteed on a predictable schedule (7 days), satisfying the erasure requirement.
- Accidental and coerced deletions are recoverable during the window via a token the user receives by email.
- The flow needs a reliable scheduled job (cron) and an email channel; both already exist for the platform.
- **Trade-off:** data persists for up to 7 days after a deletion request. This is the deliberate price of reversibility.
